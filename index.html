The Rigidity Problem

Understanding what happens when relationships get stuck

When Conflict Happens, We Get RIGID

Common Reactions:

  • Defend our position
  • Attack or criticize
  • Shut down or withdraw
  • Pursue anxiously
  • Go numb

The Result:

  • Stuck in patterns
  • Can't adapt or respond
  • Lose contact with what matters
  • Disconnect from ourselves and others
  • Experiential avoidance

Ask the group: "When's a time you got stuck in a relationship pattern? What happened in your body?"

GIVE Skills Create Flexibility

Not just "being nice" - engaging psychological processes that prevent rigidity

GIVE = Building Relational Psychological Flexibility

Openness Process

G - GENTLE

Creates safety for difficult content to be present without triggering shutdown

Mechanism: Signals "this is safe" → both people can stay out of fight/flight → openness instead of defensiveness
Awareness Process

I - INTERESTED

Tracks the other person's experience in real-time, moment-to-moment

Mechanism: Present-moment tracking of their ACTUAL experiencing → not reacting to assumptions → contact with reality as it unfolds
Openness Process

V - VALIDATE

Accepts their experience without needing it to change

Mechanism: "That makes sense" = radical acceptance → teaches them they don't have to hide parts of themselves → prevents experiential avoidance
Flexibility Process

E - EASY MANNER

Prevents getting fused/stuck in seriousness and intensity

Mechanism: Lightness creates defusion → space from the content → maintains adaptive response range → flexibility instead of rigidity

The Teaching: "So we're not just learning 'be nice' - we're learning how to stay psychologically flexible when your nervous system wants to go rigid. These skills engage the core mechanisms that create change: staying aware, staying open, and maintaining flexibility even when it's uncomfortable."

How GIVE Skills Work in Your Nervous System

Understanding the mechanisms underneath the behaviors

When you're GENTLE:

You're signaling "this is safe" - which means both of you can stay out of fight/flight. That's the openness process - you're creating space where difficult stuff can be present without triggering shutdown. Without gentleness → threat activation → experiential avoidance (shut down, attack, flee). With gentleness → safety → willingness to stay present with difficult content.

When you're INTERESTED:

You're tracking what's actually happening for them moment-to-moment. Not what you assume, not what you want them to feel - what IS. That's the awareness process - staying in contact with reality as it unfolds. You're noticing: Are they constricted? Open? Defensive? Softening? This prevents you from relating to a concept of the person and keeps you relating to their actual experiencing.

When you VALIDATE:

You're showing that their experience doesn't need to be different for connection to happen. That's radical acceptance - the opposite of experiential avoidance. "That makes sense" = "Your experience doesn't need to change for us to proceed." You're teaching them: "You don't have to hide parts of yourself to be here with me." This directly counters the "you shouldn't feel that way" response that creates shutdown.

When you have EASY MANNER:

You're preventing fusion - that thing where you get so stuck in the content that you lose flexibility. When things get too intense/serious → fusion with content → rigidity. Lightness/humor creates defusion - a little space from the intensity so you can respond adaptively. Easy manner signals: "We can hold this lightly rather than grip it tightly." This maintains your adaptive response range.

The Bridge: "Ready to feel what this is like in your body? We're going to practice these as lived experiences, not just concepts."

Round 1: Embodied Noticing

Feeling GIVE skills in your body

Guided Exploration (10 minutes)

The facilitator will guide you through a body-based exploration of what these skills feel like physically. Everyone stays together, cameras optional.

  1. GENTLE: Think of a time someone was harsh with you. Notice what happens in your body (tightness? heat? contraction?). Now think of a time someone was gentle. Notice what shifts. Where do you feel "gentle" in your body? Your chest? Face? Shoulders? What happens to your breath?
  2. INTERESTED: Remember a conversation where someone wasn't really listening - they were waiting to talk. Notice that feeling. Now remember when someone was genuinely curious about you. What's different? What does "being interesting" feel like to receive?
  3. VALIDATE: Think of a time someone said "you shouldn't feel that way." Notice the impact. Now think of someone saying "that makes complete sense." Feel the difference. Where does validation land in your body?
  4. EASY MANNER: Embody rigid seriousness - tense your jaw, furrow your brow, grip your hands. Hold it. Now release and soften - uncross your arms, soften your face, breathe. Notice the shift in your nervous system.

Brief Shares (5 minutes)

Popcorn style - whoever wants to speak:

  • ◆ "What did you notice in your body?"
  • ◆ "What surprised you?"
  • ◆ "Which skill had the strongest physical sensation?"

Round 2: Creative Expression

Making GIVE skills visible and tangible

Create Something (15 minutes)

Grab something to write or draw with. Choose ONE of these prompts and spend 5 minutes creating. This isn't about art quality - it's about externalizing your experience.

Option 1

Draw/write what "validation" looks like or feels like

Option 2

Write the harshest thing your inner critic says, then write how you'd validate that part

Option 3

Use objects around you to create a little scene of "easy manner" vs. "rigidity"

Option 4

Draw the difference between "interested" and "checked out" using colors/shapes

Round 3: Live Practice

Practicing GIVE skills in real-time together

Group Practice (20 minutes)

We'll practice these skills together through live demonstration and group participation. Facilitator demonstrates first, then volunteers practice.

INTERESTED Practice

  1. Facilitator demonstrates: "I'm going to share something for 60 seconds. Your job is to show me you're interested through your face, body, and energy - even though we're on screens. Ready?"
  2. Group observes: What did you notice the facilitator doing to show interest?
  3. Volunteers practice: 2-3 people volunteer to share something for 60 seconds each. Everyone else practices showing interest.
  4. Debrief: Sharers - what did you notice when people were present? Listeners - what was hard? What felt natural?

VALIDATE Practice

  1. Setup: Facilitator asks for a volunteer to share a frustration (mild, not traumatic)
  2. Group practices: Everyone unmutes and practices validation responses:
    • "That makes complete sense because..."
    • "I can see why you'd feel..."
    • "Of course you'd react that way..."
  3. Notice: Sharer - what did validation feel like to receive? Group - where was it hard to validate instead of fix/advise?

Integration Questions

  • ◆ What did you notice in YOUR body when you were really present vs. checked out?
  • ◆ Which skill felt most natural? Which was hardest?
  • ◆ What surprised you about practicing on Zoom vs. in-person?

Session Summary: GIVE Skills

What We Learned

GIVE skills build relational psychological flexibility by engaging core change mechanisms: staying aware of what's happening (INTERESTED), staying open to difficult content (GENTLE, VALIDATE), and maintaining flexibility (EASY MANNER). These aren't just "nice behaviors" - they're processes that prevent rigidity in relationships.

What We Practiced

We explored GIVE skills through three experiential rounds: embodied noticing (feeling the skills in our bodies), creative expression (making them visible), and live practice (using them in real-time). Each round built on the last, moving from internal awareness to external action.

The Mechanisms

  • GENTLE creates openness by signaling safety → prevents defensive shutdown
  • INTERESTED maintains awareness by tracking real-time experiencing → prevents reacting to assumptions
  • VALIDATE enables openness by accepting without requiring change → prevents experiential avoidance
  • EASY MANNER maintains flexibility by preventing fusion → preserves adaptive responding

Practice This Week

Notice one relationship interaction where you could experiment with GIVE skills. Don't aim for perfection - aim for awareness. What happens in your body when you practice gentleness? When you really track someone's experience? When you validate without fixing? Bring your observations to next week's group.
